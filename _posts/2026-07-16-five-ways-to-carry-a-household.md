In January, fifteen women in Kole District spent two weeks photographing their days. The images that came back from our photovoice research were not about climate change in the abstract. They were about the walk for water getting longer. About fuelwood journeys that eat the morning. About children needing more minding in drought months, and about tree-planting meetings a woman cannot attend because there is no one else to cook.

At the baraza where these photographs were presented — 173 people at Apii Primary School, caregivers and local leaders together — something shifted. As one Natural Resources Committee member put it: if we want the community to protect the forest, we must also support the people caring for families.

We left that meeting with two kinds of evidence. Stories, which move people. And a monitoring panel — 80 households answering structured monthly interviews on tablets — which produces numbers, which move budgets. And we ran straight into the problem every small organisation knows: the stories and the numbers live in different chapters of the report. The stories say who. The numbers say how many. Neither says both.

Then we read a piece by the design research studio Quicksand — Personas you can count — about connecting qualitative personas of women's digital lives to national survey data. Their move was almost defiantly simple: instead of running a clustering algorithm or training a classifier, they hand-wrote the rules that assign every survey respondent to a persona. Deterministic. Legible. Arguable. The thresholds came from two years of fieldwork, and any sceptical stakeholder could open the rule set and pick a fight with it.

We borrowed the whole idea and scaled it down — from a national survey to an 80-household panel.
